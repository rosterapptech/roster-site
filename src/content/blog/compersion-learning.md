In monogamous relationships there is no common word for it: the warm feeling that arises when your partner is happy with another person. In the polyamorous world it is called **compersion** – and it is often considered the emotional counterpart to jealousy.

The good news: compersion is not a personality trait that some people have and others do not. It is a skill you can practise and develop. This article shows you how.

## What exactly is compersion?

Compersion describes the joy and warmth you feel when someone you love is made happy by another relationship. Some describe it as "co-joy" – similar to how you might feel happy for a close friend who has just fallen in love, only directed towards your own partner.

Important: compersion is not a requirement and not proof of whether you are "good enough" at polyamory. Most people feel compersion sometimes and jealousy other times – sometimes even simultaneously. Feeling both at once is completely normal.

## Why does compersion feel hard at first?

We are culturally trained to see love as a scarce resource: if my partner gives time and affection to someone else, less remains for me. This "scarcity thinking" gets in the way of compersion. The first step is therefore a cognitive one: recognising that your partner's happiness does not take anything away from you. Love does not work like a cake where every slice that goes out leaves less behind.

## 5 ways to strengthen compersion

### 1. Start with the easy moments

Compersion rarely appears first in the most difficult situation. Notice the moments where it already comes naturally – when your partner returns glowing from a lovely date, for example. Consciously registering these moments builds the feeling gradually.

### 2. Connect your partner's happiness to your own

When you love someone, you want them to do well. Compersion is essentially just this logic taken to its conclusion: their happiness is your concern too – regardless of where it comes from.

### 3. Work on your own foundation

Compersion comes more easily when you feel secure in yourself. Your own fulfilling relationships, hobbies, friendships and a stable sense of self-worth are the soil in which co-joy grows.

### 4. Talk about it

Ask your partner to share their positive experiences with you – to the extent that feels right for both of you. For many people, compersion becomes more tangible when they witness their partner's happiness rather than just imagining it.

### 5. Write down your moments

Compersion and jealousy often alternate. When you note both feelings down, you can see over time that the moments of co-joy increase – and that is enormously encouraging. ## Compersion and jealousy are not opponents

It is tempting to see compersion as the "end goal" and [jealousy](/blog/jealousy-polyamory) as failure. It is more helpful to understand both as two sides of the same emotional work. Jealousy shows you a need; compersion shows you your connectedness. Dealing honestly with jealousy often creates the space in which compersion can grow at all.

## Common questions

**Can you learn compersion, or do you either have it or not?**
You can learn it. Compersion is a skill that grows with practice, self-confidence and experience.

**Is it bad if I do not feel compersion?**
No. Compersion is a wonderful bonus feeling, but not a prerequisite for successful polyamory. Honest engagement with your own feelings counts for more.

**Can I feel compersion and jealousy at the same time?**
Yes, in fact this is very common. Feelings are not either-or.

**What is the difference between compersion and empathy?**
Empathy relates to someone else's pain or difficulty. Compersion is specifically the joy felt over a loved one's happiness in another relationship.
