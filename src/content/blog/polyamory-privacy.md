Relationship data is among the most sensitive there is: who is with whom, sexual health, boundaries, intimate details, the [polycule](/blog/what-is-a-polycule) as a whole. In polyamory this concerns not just you, but also your partners and their metamours. All the more surprising how rarely privacy is discussed in this context.

This article explains why privacy matters especially in non-monogamous relationships – and how to genuinely protect your data.

## Why privacy carries particular weight in polyamory

Not everyone lives openly polyamorous. Many people are careful, for good reasons, about who they come out to – because of family, work, custody situations, or social pressure. A data leak or an overly curious tool can be more than just embarrassing; it can have real consequences.

On top of that: in polyamory you are often managing not just your own information but also that of people who never agreed to have their data stored anywhere. When you save the sexual health status, boundaries, or even just the existence of a metamour somewhere, you carry responsibility for someone else's privacy too.

## The typical weak points

**Cloud services and accounts:** Many apps require an account and store everything on their servers. You have to trust that the company operates securely, does not get hacked, and does not analyse or sell your data.

**Tracking and advertising:** Free apps often fund themselves through data. With a topic this sensitive, that is a risk worth being conscious of.

**Notes apps and messengers:** Convenient, but often synced to the cloud and rarely end-to-end encrypted. Shared devices or family accounts add another layer of risk.

**Shared calendars:** Handy for organising, but anyone with access potentially sees more than everyone is comfortable with.

## What to look for in tools and apps

1. **Local storage instead of forced cloud.** Data that never leaves your device cannot be leaked from someone else's server. A "local-first" approach is the strongest protection.
2. **No mandatory account.** No account means no central database that can be hacked.
3. **No tracking, no third-party analytics.** Check whether the app is transparent about not sharing user behaviour with third parties.
4. **Optional, encrypted sync.** If you want to sync across multiple devices, this should run via an encrypted service that only you can access.
5. **Device protection.** An additional lock via Face ID, Touch ID or a PIN protects you if someone picks up your phone.

## Privacy is respect – for your partners too

In the end, data protection in polyamory is not just about you. Handling the information of your partners and metamours with care is a form of respect and trust. Choosing a tool that shares as little data as possible from the outset protects not only yourself but everyone who belongs to your polycule.

## Common questions

**Are polyamory apps with accounts insecure?**
Not automatically, but every account on someone else's server is an additional risk. Local-first apps without an account avoid this risk from the start.

**What does "local-first" mean?**
That your data is stored primarily on your own device and does not leave it, rather than landing in a cloud by default.

**How do I protect my relationship data most easily?**
Choose tools with local storage, activate a device or app lock, and be cautious with cloud sync and shared accounts.

**Am I allowed to store information about metamours in an app?**
This is a question of respect and, depending on your country, also of data protection law. Choose tools with local storage so this information stays exclusively on your device.
